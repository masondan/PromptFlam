# PromptFlam Editorial Style Guide

**For journalists writing with AI assistance. British English conventions.**

---

## Overview

This style guide synthesises principles from Reuters, BBC, and Guardian journalism standards. It ensures AI-assisted content maintains editorial integrity, clarity, and consistency.

**Apply these rules to all AI-generated drafts, summaries, and headlines.**

---

## 1. Accuracy & Verification

**The Cardinal Rule:** Verify all claims. Accuracy always beats speed.

### Sourcing
- **Name your sources.** Identified sources are more credible than anonymous ones.
- Always attribute facts: "According to X," "The report states," "Government data shows."
- If information comes from a single source and is significant, flag it: "A single source tells us…"
- Be explicit about what you don't know: "It's unclear whether…," "Details remain unconfirmed."
- When using AI-generated content, fact-check outputs against known sources before publishing.

### Reflecting Reality
- Don't sensationalise or exaggerate. A "surge" in numbers should reflect the actual scale.
- Avoid loaded language that skews reality: "flood of migrants" vs "arrival of."
- Be precise with verbs. Use "stated," "reported," "indicated," not "claimed" or "insisted" (which imply judgment).

### Corrections
- Errors must be corrected promptly and visibly. Never bury corrections in later stories.
- Acknowledge the mistake clearly: "We reported incorrectly that… The correct figure is…"

---

## 2. Clear, Direct Language

**Principle:** Write so a reasonably educated reader understands on first reading.

### Structure
- **Lead with the most important fact** (inverted pyramid). The reader should know the news in the first paragraph.
- Provide context without burying the headline: "This matters because…"
- Use short sentences. Aim for under 20 words per sentence.
- Break complex ideas into numbered or bulleted lists.

### Word Choice (British English)
- Use active voice: "The government announced" not "it was announced by the government."
- Prefer common words. Avoid jargon unless you explain it first.
- Use British spelling: colour, orgI will review anisation, realise, travelled, licence (noun), license (verb).
- Favour short words: "use" not "utilise," "help" not "assistance," "end" not "terminate."

### Tone
- Write for your audience's knowledge level. Don't patronise; don't assume expertise.
- Be conversational but professional. Contractions (we're, it's) are acceptable if they fit the tone.
- Avoid exclamation marks. Use them sparingly, only for genuine surprise or emphasis.
- Don't inject opinion: use neutral verbs. "The minister said" not "the minister admitted" or "revealed."

---

## 3. Attribution & Sources

**Principle:** Readers must know how you know what you know.

### How to Attribute
- Place attribution early or at the point of the fact: "The Office for National Statistics reports that unemployment rose 2%."
- For direct quotes: always name the speaker and their role. "John Smith, Chief Executive of X, said: '…'"
- For paraphrased ideas: "According to Smith, the figure is incorrect."

### Handling Unnamed Sources
- Avoid unnamed sources unless absolutely necessary.
- If used, explain the source's position: "A government insider told us," "A whistleblower familiar with the situation said."
- Single unnamed sources should be exceptional. Use multiple sources whenever possible.

### Third-Party Material
- Clearly label video, audio, or images not gathered by you: "Reuters footage shows…," "Government handout image."
- Credit rival news organisations: "As the BBC reported last week…"

---

## 4. Fairness & Balance

**Principle:** Represent perspectives without false equivalence.**

### Balance ≠ Equal Time
- Don't give equal weight to well-evidenced facts and minority opinions.
- On controversial subjects, include multiple perspectives, but weight them by credibility and evidence.
- Provide fair opportunity for reply: "We approached the minister for comment but received no response."

### Avoiding Bias
- Beware loaded words: "controversial," "shocking," "allegedly," "surprisingly."
- Don't use language suggesting you're taking sides: "fail" (as in "failed to comment"), "insist," "demand."
- Watch for unconscious assumptions. What might seem neutral to you may carry bias.

### Accountability
- Explain the "so what?" for the reader: why this story matters to them.
- Give context: historical background, relevant comparisons, future implications.

---

## 5. Audience-Centred Writing

**Principle:** Write for the reader, not the institution.**

### Know Your Audience
- Consider their knowledge level, interests, and location.
- Explain acronyms on first use: "The Office for National Statistics (ONS) reported…"
- Avoid insider jargon. If you must use technical terms, explain them.

### Inclusivity
- Use "people with X" not "X victims" or "sufferers from X." Agency matters.
- Avoid stereotypes or assumptions about groups.
- Where relevant, include diverse voices and perspectives.

### Impact
- Lead with what affects readers: "Petrol prices will rise 5p per litre" not "global crude oil markets respond to geopolitical tensions."
- Show relevance: "This affects you because…"

## 6. British English Conventions

### Spelling
- colour, honour, favour, labour
- centre, theatre, metre
- realise, analyse, organisation, emphasise
- travelling, cancelled, focused (one l before -ed)

### Usage
- Use the Oxford comma sparingly; The Guardian/BBC style often omits it: "tea, coffee and biscuits" not "tea, coffee, and biscuits."
- Single quotes for quotes: 'He said, "I disagree."'
- Avoid "would have" used loosely; "might have" is often better.
- British dates: 3 March 2026 or 3 Mar 2026 (no commas).
- **Avoid Americanisms:** "autumn" not "fall," "flat" not "apartment," "programme" not "program."

### Numbers & Measurements
- Spell out numbers one to nine; use figures from 10 onwards (except at sentence start).
- Use metric: kilometres, metres, kilograms. Include imperial in brackets if helpful: "5 miles (8km)."
- For UK audience, assume pence/pounds; for international, clarify currency.

## 7. Practical Tips for AI-Generated Content

### Before Publishing
1. **Read aloud.** Does it sound natural?
2. **Fact-check.** Verify all claims, dates, figures, names.
3. **Check tone.** Does it match your publication's voice?
4. **Remove jargon.** Replace technical terms or explain them.
5. **Verify sources.** Ensure attributions are accurate and complete.

### Common AI Issues to Watch
- **Hallucinations:** AI may invent plausible-sounding but false facts. Always verify.
- **Passive voice creep:** AI loves "it is said that…" Rewrite actively.
- **Over-explanation:** AI sometimes over-contextualises. Trim unnecessary background.
- **Tone inconsistency:** AI may shift tone mid-draft. Smooth transitions.
- **Missing nuance:** AI may flatten complex issues. Add layers of context.

---

## 8. British Stylistic Preferences

### Headlines
- Use present tense for immediacy: "Minister announces new policy" not "Minister announced."
- Avoid alliteration and puns unless they're genuinely clever.
- Favour clarity over cleverness.

### Quotes
- Use them sparingly. A few powerful quotes beat many weak ones.
- Embed short quotes: He said policy was "a disaster."
- Use block quotes (indented) only for extended quotes (50+ words).

### Hyphenation (British style)
- Use hyphens in compound adjectives before a noun: "well-known writer," "full-time job."
- No hyphen after adverbs ending in -ly: "clearly stated plan," not "clearly-stated plan."

---

## 9. What NOT to Do

- ❌ Don't sensationalise.
- ❌ Don't hide sources or corrections.
- ❌ Don't assume reader knowledge.
- ❌ Don't use loaded verbs to signal opinion.
- ❌ Don't report rumours as fact without verification.
- ❌ Don't give equal weight to unequal evidence.
- ❌ Don't use American spelling or conventions without noting it's a direct quote.

---

## Summary: The PromptFlam Pledge

When you use AI to draft a story:

1. ✓ **Verify** every fact.
2. ✓ **Attribute** all sources clearly.
3. ✓ **Clarify** what you don't know.
4. ✓ **Write clearly**—short sentences, active voice, British English.
5. ✓ **Balance** perspectives fairly; weight by evidence, not equality.
6. ✓ **Explain why** it matters to your audience.
7. ✓ **Correct** errors promptly and visibly.
